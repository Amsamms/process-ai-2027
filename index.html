The quiet gap

Over the past two years, software developers learned to work alongside AI coding assistants. Their productivity changed shape. Their colleagues felt it.

Process and petroleum engineers did not.

The reason is not the technology. It is the framing. Every guide, every tutorial, every demo of these tools is wrapped around a source-code repository. A refinery engineer does not have a source-code repository. They have operating manuals, P and IDs, vendor drawings, datasheets, monthly trend reports, and a deep email archive. None of those live in Git, so the engineer concludes the tool is not for them.

This site shows the reframe in practice. One process engineer, working at a refinery in operation, built an AI setup that treats his own documents, drawings, and emails as the codebase. The setup runs on a single free cloud machine. Anyone with no programming background can copy it.

LAYER 01

The Library

The technical books, manuals, and reference drawings that an engineer would normally open one at a time. The AI reads them all.

Every refinery engineer carries a private bookshelf in their head. Operating manuals from the licensor. The unit's own manuals from before and after revamps. Pages and pages of DCS screen drawings. Years of operator work instructions. A handful of reference books, dog-eared.

Indexing this bookshelf into the AI took a single weekend. The result is a structured library that the AI consults before ever reaching for the open internet. About 6.3 gigabytes, organised into seven topic folders. The full library is sketched on the right.

Before
An engineer searching for the recommended chloride injection setpoint at end-of-run conditions opens four large PDFs in sequence, uses Ctrl+F across each, copies fragments into a notepad, and rebuilds the answer from memory. Forty-five minutes on a good day.
After
The same engineer types one plain English question. The AI returns the exact page references from the licensor manual, the corresponding section of the unit-specific manual, and a short summary of the difference between the two. Forty seconds.

LAYER 02

The Inbox Memory

Two decades of work email plus every attachment ever sent, turned into a single memory the AI can search by meaning.

Most knowledge in a refinery does not live in manuals. It lives in email. A vendor's quiet warning four years ago about a heat exchanger. A correspondence chain about catalyst loading that touches twelve people across two companies. A photograph attached to a pump-vibration complaint from 2019.

The engineer mirrors his work inbox into a personal pipeline. Every message and every attachment (PDF, Word, Excel) is converted to searchable text and indexed by meaning, not just by keyword. A daily background job keeps it current. About sixty one gigabytes of mail and attachments, queryable by a single plain English question.

Worked case: a critical heat exchanger
"Reconstruct the full vendor correspondence on the combined feed exchanger, with all KPI breaches, all open vendor recommendations, and the dates."
The manual way
  • Open the four work-email folders that contain vendor correspondence.
  • Read 412 messages, filter by sender, date, attachments.
  • Open every attached Excel, every PDF report.
  • Build a spreadsheet of breaches by hand.
  • Write the briefing memo for the vendor meeting.
3 working days
With the AI
  • One plain English question into the AI.
  • The AI reads all 412 messages and their attachments.
  • It returns a 12-row breach table, a chronological event timeline, and the five still-open vendor recommendations.
  • The engineer asks for a Word document. The AI writes it.

LAYER 03

The Drawing Reader

Attach a scanned P and ID. Ask a question in plain English. Get a structured engineering answer.

Process engineers spend hours on drawings. Looking for relief valves on a vessel. Checking whether a vent line is isolated. Counting the manual valves on a flare header. The new generation of AI can look at the same drawing and answer those questions directly.

To the right is a real vendor P and ID for a refrigeration package attached to a refinery unit. Identifying labels (vendor name, drawing number, unit number, tag numbers) have been removed for this public page. The drawing itself is unchanged.

The conversation
"Look at the attached P and ID. Tell me, in four bullets, the safety concerns I should review before signing off."
The AI answered in 90 seconds
  1. The receiver vessel carries two pressure relief valves in parallel. Confirm that at least one is online and that the bypass valve between them is car-sealed in the correct position.
  2. The vent line from the stabiliser overhead does not show a normally-closed isolation valve before tying into the relief header. Verify against the latest red-line update.
  3. The level transmitter on the suction drum has no visible high-high level interlock to trip the downstream pump. Cross-check with the cause-and-effect document.
  4. Two heat exchangers are shown with a shared common drain header. Confirm that the cross-routing cannot allow back-flow of one fluid into the other process side during isolation for maintenance.
All four points were cross-referenced against API recommended practices and the unit's own work-instruction set. The AI cited the relevant section numbers for each.
THE RECIPE

Five steps to build your own engineer's AI.

No programming background needed. Each step is something a working engineer can do in an hour or less.

  1. 01
    Get a free cloud machine.

    Sign up for the free tier of any major cloud provider. The cheapest plan is enough. The one this engineer used costs zero per month, forever, and gives twelve gigabytes of memory.

    Oracle Always Free
  2. 02
    Install the AI on it.

    One command in the terminal. The AI lives on your cloud machine the way a junior engineer would, with permission to read files and run small scripts.

    Claude Code documentation
  3. 03
    Point it at your documents.

    Upload your manuals, drawings, screens, and work instructions to a single folder on the cloud machine. Tell the AI to organise it. It will write a one-page index that lists everything by topic, by unit, and by date.

  4. 04
    Mirror your work email.

    Set up an automatic forward from your work inbox to a personal address that the AI can read. The AI will index every message and every attachment for you on a daily schedule. No data leaves your control.

  5. 05
    Ask your first question.

    Try: "Look across my library and my mail. Tell me everything we know about the last failure of equipment X. Reply as a one-page memo I can send to my manager." The first answer will surprise you. The second will change how you work.
